The ultimate goal:¶

reduce morbidity and mortality of mosquito borne tropical diseases...¶

Why the insects rather than the people?¶

1. Effective medical interventions to dengue and malaria either do not exist or are not yet suited for use in the isolated/impoverished areas most affected.¶

Many serotypes of dengue virus exist (DENV1 - DENV5).¶

Antibodies that completely protect against one serotype actually increase the risk of serious disease when in the presence of another serotype in the same patient.¶

Vaccines must simultaneously protect against all serotypes or risk harming the patient.¶

2. Dengue fever and malaria require a mosquito to infect humans.¶

Mosquitoes have been called the deadliest animals on Earth.¶

Diseases¶

transmitted through the act of feeding on our blood

Hematophagy¶

feeding on blood¶

a trait hypothesized to be ancestral to the mosquito clade¶

A typical transmission cycle¶

1. A female mosquito bites an infected person.

2. The pathogen is taken into the midgut along with the bloodmeal.

3. The pathogen must escape the midgut to gain access to the hemolymph.

4. The pathogen must gain access to the salivary glands.

5. Infectious mosquito bites an uninfected person and the pathogen in transfered in her saliva.

Strategic "weak" points¶

1. If the pathogen is unable to escape the midgut, the insect remains essentially uninfected.

2. If the pathogen is unable to access the salivary glands, the insect in unable to transmit the pathogen to another human.

  1. Dengue will infect the lining of the midgut but eventually be cleared by the mosquito's anti-sense RNA machinery

These weak points present targets for designing transgenic mosquitoes that are able to block the pathogen from reaching the next human host.¶

How does one design a transgenic mosquito to do this?¶

Elements of transgenic vector mosquito control¶

There are four fundamental elements needed for a successful transgenic vector control product

1. genomic introduction of heritable traits¶

2. development of effective anti-pathogen gene construct¶

3. genetic control of expression pattern of gene construct¶

4. strategy to bring gene construct to near-fixation frequencies in native populations¶

My work will improve our ability to evaluate and design the control of spaciotemporal expression patterns of gene constructs.¶

How much depth should i take the 4 elements in this talk?

Genetic control of expression patterns¶

How does the genome determine gene expression patterns?

Through cis-regulatory elements working together as cis-regulatory modules

CREs and CRMs

How does this work?

  • CREs made up of individual regions of DNA sequence conforming to a TFBS PWM illustrated by the colored boxes in -A-
    • the TFBS PWM denoted by the LOGO
  • A CRM is constructed of binding sites for multiple cooperative TFs that interact to define the conditions required for expression or repression for the gene(s) under its influence
  • -A- denotes only the sequence binding sites while -B- adds the TFs and other cooperative elements

Typical method for characterizing CRE and CRM activity by experimentation: promoter bashing.

Promoter bashing¶

Promoter bashing is impractical in most mosquito systems.

  • Generating a single transgenic line with genomic integrations takes months and is very labor intensive.
  • Promoter bashing methods require the ability to place altered versions of a promoter in the same genomic context or location to accurately interpret the results.

Without a way to predict which CREs/CRMs may be important in a promoter region intended for use in controlling a transgene, each candidate promoter remains a black box.¶

Black boxes are fine...¶

as long as they work perfectly¶

the first time.¶

And if it works 80% as well as needed?¶

With no idea of what to adjust¶

the promoter is usually abandoned and the process starts all over.¶

There must be another way to predict active CREs and CRMs of promoters in non-model systems...¶

Promoter bashing works by choosing one gene and asking how many altered versions of its promoter affect the expression profile¶

Modern high throughput sequencing allows us to measure the expression profiles of virtually every gene in the genome at once¶

If we collected every other expression profile in the genome that is similar to our original target profile¶

we could then search all the associated promoter sequences for what they have in common¶

similar expression profile does not necessarily require similar regulatory elements¶

use other information to attempt to filter as much of that noise out of the gene/promter sets¶

Approach¶

Goal: Establish a method for grouping promoters to aid computational CRE/CRM discovery.¶

In other words: form groupings containing as few "incorrect" members as possible.¶

What can be done to limit noise in the analysis?¶

  • Focus on expression profiles related to bloodfeeding in one tissue [midgut]
  • Take advantage of the hypothesized shared ancestry of hematophagy
  • Compare promoter sequences for similarities of the presence of motifs that fit models of previously described CREs
  • Compare promoter sequences for similarities of predicted functional overlap

How to evaluate the resulting groupings...¶

Withold analysis of predicted functional overlap until the groupings have been formed.¶

Putative Regulatory Mechanisms¶

Correlation of CRE scanning signature between ortholog pairs¶

scanning signature:¶

for a set of defined CRE motif models...

  • record all regions of a promoter that fit a given CRE model better than expected given the background sequences
  • weight each site by its positive log odds score
  • sum the weighted sites for each CRE model in a single promoter sequence
  • this gives each gene's promoter a vector of the counts for each CRE model weighted by the quality of each site (scanning signature)
  • pairs of promoter scanning signatures can be compared to generate a score representing the similarity of the CRE make-up of the two

gFunc: graph-based functional genomics framework¶

based on an existing, well maintained network-graph framework called networkx¶

uses networkx as a graph-database backbone¶

gFunc provides code for installing common genomic data-types into the graph structure¶

The graph model¶

based on the 3-way, 1:1 ortholog-relationships¶

The Phylogenetic Transcriptional Correlation Index¶

$\mathrm{PTCI} = \left( r_{x} + \frac{r_{t}}{2} \right) \cdot w(d)$
¶

attempts to provide a metric that

  • combines multiple pairwise Pearson correlation coefficients ($r$)
    • relating to multiple comparison dimensions for each ortholog-pair
    • $r_{x}$ is correlation of mAPs
    • $r_{t}$ is correlation of CRE scanning signatures
  • weights it by the relative evolutionary divergence times of each pairwise comparison ($d$)
  • $w$ is a scaling function that scales $d$ from millions of years to between $1.0$ and $1.1$
  • 1 - 1.0 used to allow the evolutionary distance to affect the ranking of an ortholog-set without having a dominating effect.
  • I explored other constraint settings, but this range appeared to be a good compromise (not shown).

$r_{t}$ is a comparison of non-experimental data¶

it should not be "depended on" as much as $r_{x}$¶

$r_{x} + \frac{r_{t}}{2}$
¶

Big picture again¶

Three step process:¶

1. PTCI calculations for all 3-way ortholog sets¶

2. Estimate a false discovery rate (FDR) by recalculating the PTCI many times with randomly assigned 3-way ortholog memberships¶

3. Retain 3-way orthologs with combined PTCI greater than or equal to FDR threshold¶

a general set of 126 insect CRE models¶

a focused set of 12 CRE models¶

Argot2¶

Falda et al. (2012). Argot2: a large scale function prediction tool relying on semantic similarity of weighted Gene Ontology terms. BMC bioinformatics doi:10.1186/1471-2105-13-S4-S14

The process:¶

The 930 protein sequences are compared to databases of well characterized proteins...¶

Our sequences are compared using peptide BLAST against the SwissProt protein sequence database¶

annotated by hand with evidence types known¶

most with at least some experimental evidence¶

They are also compared using HMMER against the Pfam database of functional domains¶

uses hidden Markov models of annotated functional domains¶

built from many instances of each domain¶

The results are sent to the Argot2 servers to be mapped onto the Gene Ontology (GO) graph of hierarchical terms¶

Disclaimer...¶

the following is a bit of an oversimplification...¶

The GO terms of the SwissProt/Pfam entries that best match each of our proteins are used to generate a total score (TS) for each term in the GO hierarchy as they relate to our proteins¶

The hierarchy is then pruned, leaving the "best" terms¶

The Argot2 developers suggest a TS threshold of around 200¶

Argot2 was the highest performer in a recent "blinded" review of functional annotation methods¶

Radivojac et al. (2013). A large-scale evaluation of computational protein function prediction. Nature methods, 10(3), 221–7. doi:10.1038/nmeth.2340

760 of 930 proteins where assigned at least one GO term with a TS of at least 200¶

452 of 930 proteins (about half) where assigned at least one GO term with a TS of at least 2000¶
